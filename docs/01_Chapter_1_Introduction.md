1.  <span id="_Toc229233509" class="anchor"></span>Chapter 1:
    Introduction

The end-of-life tyres (ELTs) are presently dangerous environmental and
general problems due to their large quantity and chemical structure
**(Han et al., 2024)**. Expired tyres are non-biodegradable materials
and present a sizable environmental risk due to their complex chemical
composition and toxic compounds that can leach into soil and water.
Pyrolysis is one process to extract energy potential products from waste
tyres **(Parthasarathy et al., 2016)**. It can decompose WT completely,
and its products such as oil, gas, carbon black and steel wire can be
used **(Han et al., 2023)**. A simulated flowsheet model of a waste tyre
pyrolysis process is presented in this paper **(Cao et al., 2023**).
This topic is considered one of the most important issues in the
contemporary world, especially considering the growing interest in the
concept of sustainability in current societies. This research focuses on
simulating the tyre recycling process by conducting a feasibility study
and analyzing it using simulation models, with the aim of evaluating the
efficiency of this type of industry and highlighting its environmental
and economic feasibility. <span dir="rtl"></span>The article proposes an
approach for simulation studies using discrete-event simulation during
the concept phase of factory planning, which “helps to control and
reduce manufacturing costs **(Schäfer et al., 2024**). Discrete event
simulation (DES) is a well-recognized method in assessing the
performance of flexible manufacturing systems (FMS), and ‘what if?’
scenarios are orchestrated **(Naim & Hoh, 1993)**.

The point of this experimental study, process simulation and economic
analysis is to assess the applicability of pyrolysis technology
**(Kibria et al., 2024)**. The results pointed out the feasibility and
sustainability of operating a 3.5 tons per day batch waste tyre
pyrolysis plant **(Nkosi et al., 2020)**. Whereas the concept of
pyrolysis process had been investigated previously by researchers in
aspects of environmental impact, chemistry, and economics, a need
existed for conducting the study using operational models as well as
feasibility analysis with respect to engineering and economic
feasibility. Such investigation would require more than simply
considering the engineering aspect of the process output; rather, the
system's flow and limitations, as well as the utilization and economic
feasibility of the process, would have to be considered.

Consequently, the aim of this study is to construct an integrated
framework for the analysis of the proposed waste tyre pyrolysis plant by
means of discrete event simulation and feasibility study. This study
will attempt to analyze the performance of the system, detect any
bottlenecks in operation, and measure its economic viability. The use of
both simulation and feasibility analysis will help us understand the
project better. In one way, simulations can assist in assessing process
flow, equipment use, and efficiency. On the other hand, through
feasibility analysis, it is easy to establish whether the system
proposed for the company is financially sound or not.

Significance of this study is that the results obtained from this
research can prove helpful in sustainable waste management along with
serving as a framework for decision making at the industrial level.
Results will enable investors to understand if a small-scale pyrolysis
plant for tyres can be economically viable and operationally effective.

The main area covered in this study is that of small-scale tyre
pyrolysis, and the work does not go into detailed discussion of any
reaction kinetics or further refineries. The main areas of interest are
flow diagrams, performance analysis, and economic feasibility.

The proposed research also looks into the practical monitoring element
in the pyrolysis plant that would utilize digital technologies. The
company in question, BioFuel Company, uses a mobile monitoring
application as a solution for operational tracking and monitoring of the
entire plant in real time. Such software aids the operator in observing
the state of the equipment, flow of materials, reactor's condition,
temperature and humidity parameters, required maintenance operations,
and even the current output level among other factors. As a part of this
project, the same software solution is simulated as a prototype that
represents an actual digital monitoring application. Of course, this
application is not a fully functional industrial control system but only
a prototype of the system in question. Still, such visualization allows
understanding the potential of such digital monitoring application
especially in terms of providing support to making managerial decisions
for the crucial parts of the plant like the pyrolysis reactor that is
the key bottleneck in the entire process. Thus, this prototype provides
a visual solution for monitoring such crucial parameters as the batch
size, the state of the unit, the material ratio, output distribution,
alerts, and reports.
