9.  <span id="_Toc229233558" class="anchor"></span>Chapter 9: Discussion

    1.  <span id="_Toc229233559" class="anchor"></span>**Integrated
        Analysis: Simulation, Economic, and Environmental**

It is clear from the results of the current research that the proposed
small-scale waste tyre pyrolysis system is better assessed considering
all dimensions – simulation, economic, and environmental. Every aspect
provides its unique point on the feasibility of the project.

**In terms of simulation analysis,** the proposed system is technically
feasible for the processing of waste tyres. The system's flow includes
the following elements: debeading, cutting, shredding, buffering,
batching, pyrolysis, and product separation. Simulation results suggest
that the system's preprocessing phases will provide materials for
processing continuously. Nevertheless, reactor will remain the critical
bottleneck since it will operate in batch mode with rather lengthy
processing times (about 18-23 hours).

**Speaking about economic feasibility,** it is necessary to say that the
project is expected to be profitable provided that the conditions
assumed are correct. It is planned that the total amount of capital
invested will make 700,000 JD. Daily net profit will be around 2,217
JD/day. Thus, the expected annual net profit is about 798,120 JD, which
means that the project can pay back within 0.88 years. In other words,
the project can be seen as a potentially viable enterprise.

**Environmentally speaking,** it is important to mention that the
project can be environmentally efficient. In order not to burden
landfills or burn waste tyres uncontrolledly, they are to be processed
into various products: pyrolysis oil, carbon black, steel wires, gas,
and light fuel fraction. From this point of view, the project follows
the principle of the circular economy since waste tyres will become
sources of valuable materials and energy.

2.  <span id="_Toc229233560" class="anchor"></span>**Comparison with
    Literature**

**The results obtained from this study coincide with those in the
previous literature,** which recognize waste tyre pyrolysis as an
economically beneficial approach to waste recycling. Several papers have
indicated that tyre pyrolysis technology can transform end-of-life tires
into various useful resources like oil, gas, carbon black, and steel
wires. The results of this study provide evidence to support this
finding since they demonstrate the capability of a small-scale plant to
produce revenues in different areas.

**Regarding the output assumptions,** they comply with the yield
distribution range described in many literature pieces on waste tyre
pyrolysis. The considered yield distribution includes about 40% of
pyrolysis oil, 30-40% carbon black, 10% gas, 10% light fuel fraction,
and 10% steel wires. They correspond to the typical yield distribution
range in a variety of studies, which implies that a small-scale plant
can be productive and generate numerous resources.

**As far as the simulation is concerned,** many publications on tyre
pyrolysis focus on thermodynamics, chemical reaction modeling, products'
compositions, and process optimization. On the contrary, this paper
provides information on the application of discrete-event simulation to
investigate flow and batching issues, queues, utilization of a
particular machine, bottlenecks, and other features of a small-scale
plant operation. It is necessary due to the nature of the studied
process, which implies both chemical process and production system.

**When compared with previous economic analysis results,** the
calculated payback period appears rather short, even though this study
uses some assumptions concerning product prices, running costs,
equipment costs, and availability. For instance, previous literature
indicated much longer payback periods at the same plant scale and
capacity. Thus, it can be concluded that economic performance heavily
depends on the specified parameters.

Therefore, the paper finds itself agreeing with literature on technical
and environmental aspects of waste tyre pyrolysis, but not with previous
economic analysis methods.

3.  <span id="_Toc229233561" class="anchor"></span>**Practical
    Implications**

The implications for practical application are evident from this
analysis. It is clear that a mini waste tyre pyrolysis plant can serve
as a promising investment option in case it is properly designed,
operated, and possesses reliable market outlets for the produced goods.

To potential investors, the preliminary economic indicators reveal that
this project can be profitable enough. The estimated payback period is
lower than one year in the considered scenario. Nevertheless, it does
not guarantee positive net present value due to product selling prices,
reliability of output generation, maintenance costs, fuel expenses,
product quality, and governmental regulations.

For plant designers, it can be stated that the reactor needs to be
focused upon in its optimization process because it is recognized as the
principal bottleneck. Consequently, the efforts can be concentrated on
such factors as reactor capacity, cycle time, loading/unloading time,
energy saving, and maintenance. The number of reactors can be increased,
or the duration of cycle time can be shortened to boost the capacity.

From the point of view of waste management authorities, this project
provides a solution for dealing with the environmental problem caused by
waste tyres. This approach ensures sustainable waste management and
implementation of the circular economy model.

Finally, to plant operators, the simulation results indicate the
importance of buffer management and batching policy selection. As is
known, the reactor operates only if there is a complete batch in it.

4.  <span id="_Toc229233562" class="anchor"></span>**Limitations of the
    Study**

However, there are several shortcomings that need to be mentioned
despite the valuable information obtained.

In the first place, the simulation model relies on assumptions regarding
its input parameters such as processing times, capacities, and other
characteristics. In a real plant, all of them may be different due to
the machinery used, tyre properties, personnel proficiency, and other
factors.

The second limitation concerns the lack of precise modeling of the
chemical reactions. The pyrolysis stage is defined as a batch process
with its time distribution, however, the reactions themselves, changes
in temperature, duration effect, catalysts' role, etc., are not taken
into account.

As far as the economic analysis is concerned, it also requires some
assumptions since the value of revenue and costs is unknown. Thus,
product prices will vary depending on the current demand, their quality,
acceptability, etc. The results of this research are only preliminary
estimates.

The fifth limitation concerns the environmental aspect. Although
environmental analysis is performed, it is conducted at the theoretical
level, which means that no complete life cycle assessment has been done.
Consequently, carbon emission reduction, energy balance, and others
cannot be calculated accurately.

Finally, the model supposes a stable operation of the system; hence, it
may be disrupted by downtime, maintenance, start-up issues, product
quality, labor problems, and so on.
