    1.  <span id="_Toc229233511" class="anchor"></span>**Waste tyres as
        an environmental and sustainability challenge**

There seems to be consensus in the literature that waste-tyres are a
sustainability challenge rather than just an environmental problem. As
stated in **Han et al. (2024)**, end-of-life tires (ELTs) represent
“critical environmental and societal problems”. Likewise, **Valentini et
al. (2022)** emphasize that “a substantial number of scrap tires
continue being discarded into landfills and stockpiled”, while **Hu et
al. (2024)** observe that modern waste-tire valorization research is
increasingly focused on “optimization processes”. Further supporting
this point of view, **Zerin et al. (2023)** highlights the constantly
growing number of ELTs, thus emphasizing the need for efficient and
sustainable recycling routes. Therefore, the scientific community agrees
that waste-tyre management is a sustainability challenge requiring
innovative and integrated technology and policy-based solutions.

Among various valorization approaches, pyrolysis is the most cited
solution in literature. According to **Han et al. (2023)**, “Pyrolysis
can decompose WT completely”, while **Parthasarathy et al. (2016)**
claim that pyrolysis allows recovering energy-rich products from waste
tires. Recent reviews also confirm the relevance and importance of
pyrolysis as one of the most promising thermochemical approaches to
waste-tire processing compared to standard disposal methods **(Bi et
al., 2022; Kumar et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025)**. Thus, the scientific
community considers pyrolysis as a key tool of creating circular economy
by reducing ELT stockpiles and producing valuable materials.

2.  <span id="_Toc229233512" class="anchor"></span>**Pyrolysis basics,
    yields, and process variables**

A major trend in literature is to define pyrolysis based on the process
conditions, output compositions, and variable sensitivity of the
process. For example, **Han et al. (2023)** point out that useful
outputs from ELT pyrolysis include “oil, gas, carbon black and steel
wire”, whereas **Zerin et al. (2023)** report typical ranges of yields
as “30–65% oil, 25–45% char and 5–20% gas”. **Parthasarathy et al.
(2016)** and Arabiourrutia et al. (2020) also suggest that product
quality and distributions are highly dependent on pyrolysis parameters.
In other words, rather than presenting pyrolysis as a process with
pre-defined outputs, researchers tend to emphasize that output
composition can be controlled by manipulating specific operating
conditions.

Variables such as temperature, particle size, catalysts and residence
time have a consistent effect on pyrolysis performance and output
quality. In particular, **Lewandowski et al. (2019)** demonstrated
significant differences in output fractions depending on reactor design,
while **Oyedun et al. (2012)** studied the effect of particle size on
pyrolysis efficiency. Moreover, **Al-Salem et al. (2022)** investigated
the impact of temperature on pyro-oil yields and quality, whereas
**Sanchís et al. (2022)** emphasized the significance of temperature
profiles during ELT pyrolysis. More recently, **Li et al. (2024)**
examined the influence of particle size on pyrolysis efficiency under
molten salt conditions, **Wang et al. (2024)** analyzed catalytic effect
on the thermodynamics and kinetics, and **Zhang et al. (2025)**
evaluated temperature and residence time influence on limonene synthesis
and transformations. All in all, the above-mentioned variables cannot be
viewed as periphery ones. Instead, they play a pivotal role in designing
a specific pyrolysis scenario and obtaining desired products.

In addition, there is a large body of literature addressing reactor
design issues and its effect on pyrolysis performance. For instance,
**Aylón et al. (2010)** modelled the operation of moving bed reactor
used for pyrolysis of waste tyres, and **Martínez et al. (2013)**
claimed that pilot-scale continuous auger reactor is feasible to use.
Furthermore, **Martínez et al. (2020)** tested a continuous twin auger
reactor for waste tire pyrolysis and described it as “roadmap toward a
circular and sustainable economy”. They continued their research and
validated intermediate pyrolysis in laboratory-scale twin auger reactors
**(Martínez et al., 2021)**. Similarly, **Qi et al. (2023)** employed a
pilot-scale screw propulsion reactor and discovered that oil
characteristics varied depending on tyre type and condensing conditions.
Importantly, the reviewed works are essential for your thesis as they
address technical details of pyrolysis, which is relevant for simulation
modeling.

3.  <span id="_Toc229233513" class="anchor"></span>**Product upgrading,
    fuel quality, and post-processing problems**

Although pyrolysis is an effective technology to recover resources,
several publications note the necessity of further upgrading the
products obtained. Thus, **Arabiourrutia et al. (2020)** advocate
catalytic pyrolysis as a means of product improvement and enhanced
selectivity. The article written by **Shehata et al. (2024)** addresses
the problem of producing diesel-like fuel from scrap tires via
sequential application of thermal and catalytic pyrolysis. Moreover,
**Farzad et al. (2021)** evaluate technoeconomic feasibility of
upgrading tire-derived pyrolysis products into limonene and fuel
fractions. The importance of these publications is in the fact that, in
addition to gross yield, economic value can depend on the extent to
which the final products may be upgraded through process engineering and
post-processing steps.

There is an issue concerning the need to lower the sulfur concentration
in waste tire pyrolysis oils and gases. The paper published by **Mello
et al. (2023)** considers the problem of pyrolysis oil and gas
desulfurization after waste tire pyrolysis. Moreover, according to
**Jiang et al. (2024)**, sulfur-containing compounds “can significantly
decrease the environmental and economic feasibility of such an
innovative solution.” The authors test various metal oxide catalysts for
simultaneous desulfurization and upgrading processes. According to the
research conducted by **Czajczyńska et al. (2024)**, gas cleaning
results in high H₂S removal efficiency through the use of amine-based
systems, thus making the use of pyrolysis gases as alternative fuels
feasible. Finally, **Seyfeli et al. (2024)** examine the possibility of
applying boron-nitride-based sorbents for sulfur removal from liquid
fuels obtained from tire pyrolysis. All these articles show that, in
addition to the ability to generate products, the capacity to improve
them and clean from contaminants plays an important role in evaluating
technological feasibility.

There is a similar issue concerning product differentiation and the
quality of recovered materials. According to **Sanchís et al. (2025)**,
the results of tire pyrolysis depend on both tire type and section,
influencing the resulting carbon quality and process sustainability.
Similarly, **Han et al. (2023)** examine the compositions, properties,
and applications of pyrolysis products, whereas **Wu et al. (2024)**
consider different modification options for recovered carbon black
through a life cycle perspective. This shows that, besides gross output
mass, other parameters, such as quality and upgradeability, should be
considered when evaluating technological solutions to scrap tires
processing.

4.  <span id="_Toc229233514" class="anchor"></span>**Process modeling,
    simulation, and the importance of discrete-event simulation**

The other important aspect studied in academic literature concerns
process modeling and simulation. For example, **Cao et al. (2023)**
explicitly mention that “A simulated flowsheet model of a waste tire
pyrolysis process is presented” in their publication, where Aspen Plus
software is used for the process reaction and separation simulation.
Earlier, the same approach was applied by Ismail and **Yang (2017)** for
developing a simulation flowsheet of tire pyrolysis process. Moreover,
**Hu et al. (2025)** simulate pyrolysis in combination with other
technological stages such as combined cooling, heat and power
generation, waste heat recovery, desalination, and carbon capture.
Hence, there is an evident trend toward wider application of simulation
modeling for analyzing thermochemical conversion processes.

However, static process modeling does not allow assessing the dynamics
of plant functioning. From the point of view of manufacturing
literature, DES is the appropriate technique for analyzing manufacturing
systems due to their complexity and stochastic nature. Specifically,
**Naim and Hoh (1993)** explain that simulation allows exploring “the
‘what if?’ scenario” of a system. Later, **Negahban and Smith (2014)**
offer a review of simulation methods in support of manufacturing systems
planning and control. Moreover, according to **Schäfer et al. (2024)**,
simulation is one of the tools useful for addressing
“cost-effectiveness” issues in concept phase of process design. Lastly,
**De Felice et al. (2025)** state that DES becomes a “key enabling
technology” for manufacturing transformation. **Chavarría-Barrientos et
al. (2018)** even propose a specific approach to guide plant design and
operation using simulation techniques. For your thesis topic, this line
of reasoning is important since a small-scale pyrolysis plant is not
merely a chemical process, but a production system characterized by
feeding lags, reactor occupation, storage capacity, utility
interactions, maintenance issues, and throughput limitations.

The majority of publications dedicated to simulation modeling of waste
tire pyrolysis concentrate on thermodynamic analysis of processes,
whereas those on DES are more related to manufacturing problems,
utilization rates, queuing, and operations. By combining these two lines
of reasoning, a research opportunity appears. Specifically, it refers to
a hybrid model that combines outputs of a process with its dynamics.
This issue is relevant to small-scale pyrolysis plants where the
limitation in equipment number highlights the problems with bottlenecks
and other system-related limitations. This gap is the conclusion derived
from the comparison between works dedicated to process simulations
**(Cao et al., 2023; Ismail & Yang, 2017; Hu et al., 2025)** and DES
**(Naim & Hoh, 1993; Negahban & Smith, 2014; Schäfer et al., 2024; De
Felice et al., 2025)**.

5.  <span dir="rtl"><span id="_Toc229301087"
    class="anchor"></span></span>**Digital monitoring and mobile-based
    operational support**

In addition to simulation modeling, many recent industrial systems are
utilizing digital monitoring applications as well to increase
operational transparency. As for waste tire pyrolysis, there are some
processes in place including feeding, pre-processing, buffering,
reactor, product separating, and maintenance controlling. Thus, an
application based monitoring system could be useful for displaying
real-time information regarding machine conditions, material levels,
reactor conditions, temperature and humidity values, alerts, as well as
other production information in a concise manner. The company BioFuel is
known to employ such monitoring applications for keeping track of the
operational condition of their industrial pyrolysis system. For this
research, we created our own monitoring application prototype to show
this idea in action. Although it does not pretend to function as an
actual industrial system, this tool can illustrate what kind of digital
monitoring interface should look like when working in tandem with
discrete-event simulation. There are two dashboards designed within this
monitoring tool: one is generalized for observing the entire process and
another one which focuses on reactor-related data including batch level,
material ratios, output ratios, alerts, reports, and maintenance issues.
