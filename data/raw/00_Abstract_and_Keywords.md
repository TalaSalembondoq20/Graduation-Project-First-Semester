# 00 Abstract and Keywords

<span id="_Toc229301079" class="anchor"></span>Abstract

This study evaluates the technical, economic, and environmental
feasibility of a small-scale waste tyre pyrolysis system using an
integrated assessment approach. The research combines discrete-event
simulation, economic feasibility analysis, and environmental impact
assessment to examine the performance and viability of the proposed
system. A simulation model was developed using Simio to represent the
main process stages, including tyre receiving, debeading, cutting,
shredding, buffer storage, batching, pyrolysis, and product separation.

The simulation model was based on production and operational data
obtained from BioFuel Company. The system assumes an average tyre weight
of 10 kg, a daily input of 1000–1500 tyres, and a daily production
capacity of 10–15 tons. Each tyre is divided into four pieces, resulting
in 4000–6000 pieces per day. The pyrolysis reactor operates as a
batch-processing unit with a capacity of 10 tons per batch and a
processing time of approximately 18–23 hours per batch.

The simulation results indicate that the preprocessing stages can
operate effectively and provide a continuous material supply to the
batching stage. However, the pyrolysis reactor was identified as the
main bottleneck because of its long batch-processing cycle. The system
can process approximately one reactor batch per day under the assumed
operating conditions.

The economic analysis shows that the project may be financially
feasible. The estimated capital investment is 700,000 JD, while the
expected net daily profit is approximately 2,217 JD/day. This results in
an expected annual net profit of approximately 798,120 JD, an expected
payback period of 0.88 years, and an expected return on investment of
about 114%. The sensitivity analysis confirms that the project remains
financially attractive under several cost and profit variation
scenarios, although reactor downtime, product prices, and operating
costs remain critical factors.

The environmental assessment indicates that the proposed system can
contribute to sustainable waste management by converting waste tyres
into useful products, including pyrolysis oil, carbon black, gas, light
fuel fraction, and steel wires. The reuse of gas as an internal energy
source can also improve energy efficiency. Overall, the study concludes
that the proposed small-scale waste tyre pyrolysis system is technically
feasible, economically promising, and environmentally beneficial, if
product quality, market availability, regulatory compliance, and reactor
reliability are properly managed.
