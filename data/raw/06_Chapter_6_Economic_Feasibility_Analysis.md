6.  <span id="_Toc229233539" class="anchor"></span>Chapter 6: Economic
    Feasibility Analysis

    1.  <span id="_Toc229233540" class="anchor"></span>**Cost Structure:
        CAPEX and OPEX**

This chapter evaluates the economic feasibility of establishing a
small-scale waste tire pyrolysis plant. The proposed system processes
waste vehicle tires and converts them into valuable products such as
diesel-like pyrolysis oil, carbon black, light fuel fraction, steel
wires, and reusable gas.

The economic assumptions are connected to the simulation model developed
in the previous chapters, where the system is designed around an average
tire weight of **10 kg**, a daily input of **10–15 tons**, and a
pyrolysis reactor capacity of **10 tons per batch**

**Market-Based CAPEX Estimation**

Diverse prices have been considered based on online sources for the cost
of pyrolysis system for tires. **Pyrolysis systems capable of processing
at a rate of 10 TPD** are available from suppliers ranging from 45,000
USD to 57,000 USD. The above prices generally do not include
installation, utilities, safety measures, etc. Marketplaces provide two
prices for 10 TPD plants, being 45,000 USD and average cost estimated at
about 57,420 USD. Additional supplier information indicates that batch
pyrolysis plants capable of producing 6-15 ton/day cost between 50,000
USD and 500,000 USD while for higher capacities, tire recycling plants
cost in the range of 300,000 USD to 1,000,000 USD.

It is worth mentioning that online supplier prices generally only
account for the central pyrolysis reactor or primary unit alone, whereas
the capital expenditure required for an industrial facility entails not
only the above factors but also those associated with the process prior
to pyrolysis, such as preparation equipment, installation,
infrastructure, etc.

From online source for currency converter and exchange rate between U.S.
Dollars (USD) to Jordanian Dinar (JOD), it seems the approximate
exchange rate for U.S. Dollars to Jordanian Dinners is **approximately 1
USD = 0.709 JOD.**

The cost of 700,000 JD thus seems quite reasonable considering that the
cost includes the following (reactor, shredder/debreading machine,
installation costs, utilities, emission control, safety measures, etc.).

<span id="_Toc229236269" class="anchor"></span>**Table 10: Estimated
CAPEX Breakdown.**

| Item                                        | Estimated Cost (JD) |
|---------------------------------------------|---------------------|
| Pyrolysis reactor / oven system             | 250,000             |
| Tire debeading, cutting, and shredding line | 120,000             |
| Condensation and oil recovery system        | 70,000              |
| Carbon black and steel wire handling        | 40,000              |
| Gas treatment and safety system             | 45,000              |
| Electrical, piping, and utilities           | 60,000              |
| Installation, commissioning, and transport  | 50,000              |
| Civil works and storage area                | 40,000              |
| Contingency and miscellaneous costs         | 25,000              |
| Total Estimated CAPEX                       | **700,000 JD**      |

Therefore, the final CAPEX of a complete waste tire pyrolysis facility
is significantly higher than the standalone reactor price reported by
online marketplaces, since a fully integrated plant requires additional
industrial infrastructure, auxiliary systems, installation, and
operational support units.

3.  <span id="_Toc229233542" class="anchor"></span>**Revenue Streams
    Analysis**

The pyrolysis process produces multiple revenue streams. Literature and
supplier sources commonly report that waste tire pyrolysis produces
pyrolysis oil, recovered carbon black, steel wire, and combustible gas.
Typical product yields are often around **40–45% oil**, **30–35% carbon
black**, **10–15% steel wire**, and **5–10% syngas**, although the exact
yield depends on tire type, reactor design, temperature, and operating
conditions.

For this project, the assumed output distribution is:

<span id="_Toc229236272" class="anchor"></span>**Table 13: The assumed
output distribution.**

| Product                | Approximate Yield |
|------------------------|-------------------|
| Diesel / pyrolysis oil | 40%               |
| Carbon black           | 30–40%            |
| Gas                    | 10%               |
| Light fuel fraction    | 10%               |
| Steel wires            | 10%               |

The daily profit values provided for the project are:

<span id="_Toc229236273" class="anchor"></span>**Table 14: The daily
profit values.**

| Product                    | Daily Profit Range (JD/day) |
|----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| Diesel / pyrolysis oil     | 1,400–2,000                 |
| Carbon black               | 500                         |
| Light fuel fraction        | 100–150                     |
| Steel wires                | 150–200                     |
| Total Daily Product Profit | **2,150–2,850**             |

Gas is not treated as direct sales revenue in this model because it is
usually reused as a heat source for the reactor. This reduces energy
consumption and improves operating efficiency rather than generating
direct cash income. Supplier sources also state that syngas can be
reused as a heat source in the pyrolysis system.

6.  <span dir="rtl"><span id="_Toc229233545"
    class="anchor"></span></span>**Break-even and Payback Period**

The payback period is calculated as:

$$\text{Payback~Period} = \frac{\text{Initial~Investment}}{\text{Annual~Net~Profit}}$$

<span id="_Toc229236274" class="anchor"></span>**Table 17: The payback
period.**

| Scenario     | Initial Investment | Annual Net Profit | Payback Period |
|--------------|--------------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Conservative | 700,000 JD         | 654,120 JD        | 1.07 years     |
| Expected     | 700,000 JD         | 798,120 JD        | 0.88 years     |
| Optimistic   | 700,000 JD         | 942,120 JD        | 0.74 years     |

This indicates that the payback period for the project will be between
**9 to 13 months** on the assumptions made. But it should be noted that
this is an estimated financial calculation. The actual payback period
can be extended because of factors such as licenses, maintenance,
downtime, quality problems, and sales.

Previously, an economic analysis of a much smaller plant with the
capacity to **process 3.5 tons** per day using the batch process
indicated feasibility but had a higher payback period of **5 years**.

7.  <span dir="rtl"><span id="_Toc229233546"
    class="anchor"></span></span>**Return on Investment**

ROI is calculated as:

$$ROI = \frac{\text{Annual~Net~Profit}}{\text{Initial~Investment}} \times 100$$

<span id="_Toc229236275" class="anchor"></span>**Table 18: The expected
ROI.**

| Scenario     | ROI     |
|--------------|---------|
| Conservative | 93.45%  |
| Expected     | 114.02% |
| Optimistic   | 134.59% |

The expected ROI is high because the daily profit assumptions are strong
compared with the assumed CAPEX. This supports the feasibility of the
project, but the model should still be treated as a preliminary
feasibility study, not a final investment decision.

The most important variables affecting feasibility are:

<span id="_Toc229302377" class="anchor"></span>**Table 19: The most

| Variable                     | Effect on Feasibility |
|------------------------------|-----------------------|
| Diesel / pyrolysis oil price | Very high impact      |

The reactor remains the most critical unit because it controls the total

batch** and requires approximately **18–23 hours**, the project is
highly sensitive to reactor availability and downtime.

9.  <span dir="rtl"><span id="_Toc229233548"
    class="anchor"></span></span>**Financial Sensitivity and Risk
    Analysis Using Python**

However, it is important to develop a model to identify whether the
waste tire pyrolysis plant project should continue or not. The model
will perform a comprehensive sensitivity and risk analysis by evaluating
the impact of increasing costs or decreasing product profit rates.
Therefore, it will be much easier to make an informed decision since the
model will analyze numerous possible outcomes of implementing this
venture.

The following assumptions were made concerning investment and operation:
the initial investment was set at 700,000 JD and the number of
operational days per month was 30. The model considers three scenarios:
conservative, expected, and optimistic. These scenarios involve changes
in daily product profit rate and monthly cost rates under diverse
economic conditions. In other words, this is a comprehensive approach
allowing identifying possible consequences of increasing or reducing
certain parameters in the economic market.

In the sensitivity section, there are several tests that determine the
effect of raising or lowering such factors as operating cost rates and
daily profit rates on the financial sustainability of the plant. For
instance, it evaluates the potential outcome of a rise in operating
costs by 10%, 20%, or even 50%. At the same time, the model performs the
opposite analysis by estimating the effect of reducing daily profit
rates by 10%, 20%, and so forth. All aspects of financial sustainability
are considered, including higher labor rates, energy consumption,
reduced prices, decreased demand, and so forth. It is evident that such
a model helps identify the weak points of the project.

The results of calculations are provided for all possible combinations,
and the following parameters are calculated: the new daily profit rates
after changes, operating costs, net profits on a daily, monthly, and
annual basis, payback period, and return on investment. Based on these
figures, the project is categorized into four categories: Highly
Feasible, Feasible, Risky, and Not Feasible. Such a classification
simplifies decision-making since investors do not need to calculate
everything manually.

The results are exported to a data table and stored in an Excel file for
further consideration. In addition, the model generates a chart for the
expected scenario. It depicts changes in monthly net profits in case of
a decline in product profit rate and an increase in operating cost
rates. Furthermore, the model defines the worst-case and best-case
scenarios of the project. In summary, the implementation of this model
facilitates evaluation.

The program calculates three possibilities in terms of finance. The
conservative assumption has lower daily profit and higher operating
costs. The expected is the most realistic possibility in the middle. The
optimistic case has higher profits in sales but lower operating costs.

This makes the program helpful because it does not give a single
definitive answer. It demonstrates how the feasibility changes based on
operating conditions.

Based on the market price searches and project assumptions, the proposed
waste tire pyrolysis plant is economically feasible. The assumed total
cost of the project at 700,000 JD is reasonable since it represents a
complete installed line, albeit higher than the basic online machine
price since it does not include installation costs, pre-processing of
materials, safety devices, civil works, and installation costs.

**The project will be profitable due to multiple revenue streams from
the output, such as diesel-like pyrolysis oil, carbon black, light fuel,
and steel wires. The assumed net income per day is around 2,217 JD/day,
translating into a projected net income of 798,120 JD/year.**

Based on the project assumptions, the expected payback period would be
around 0.88 years, while the expected return on investment (ROI) is
approximately 114%.

Nevertheless, the final investment decision shall depend on other
factors like the quality of the output products, environmental
clearance, emission control, marketing arrangements for the carbon black
and pyrolysis oil, the frequency of reactor downtime, and maintenance
costs.
